Introduction to weight stigma

Last updated: 12th February 2025

Weight stigma is the discrimination faced by people on the basis of their weight. This is also referred to as sizeism, anti-fatness, and anti-fat bias. Aubrey Gordon (2021) defines it more precisely as “the attitudes, behaviors, and social systems that specifically marginalize, exclude, underserve, and oppress fat bodies.”

Readily accessible, documented accounts of weight stigma in health services go back to at least 1983, when Doris K. shared some of her experiences as a pregnant fat woman in the United States in the book “Shadow on a Tightrope: Writings by Women on Fat Oppression”, edited by Lisa Schoenfielder and Barb Wieser (1983):

When I was pregnant with my second child, I was about fifteen pounds overweight by the charts. The doctor told me not to gain any weight with this pregnancy, so that when I delivered, I wouldn’t have the weight problem. So I tried to go along with it. I would starve myself for days, knowing that I had to go to the doctor to weigh in. I can remember a few days when I didn’t eat anything, or maybe just had a cup of coffee — that’s no way for a pregnant mother to carry a child! But still I gained five pounds. One day, after seeing that I had gained a few pounds, the doctor blew up at me. He threw the chart down. He kept shaking his head and telling me how disgusted he was with me. As he was cursing at me, I sat there crying. The tears were running down my face. To hell with me, I was nothing to him, I wasn’t a person, I was a big fat blob that happened to be carrying a baby. He finally said, “I don’t give a damn about you. But I do care about that baby. I’ll get rid of that weight.”

So he put me in a hospital and on a very low calorie diet, about 700 calories per day. And after two or three days he came in to check on me, and I hadn’t lost any weight. He said, “Who’s slipping you candy?” I said, “Honest to God, I’m not cheating.” He said, “You have to be, because you’re not losing.”

So he cut the diet down to 500 calories per day. And he had the nurses watching me to make sure I didn’t cheat, as if I was such a mess that I couldn’t even be trusted inside a hospital, let alone the real world. And after a week I had lost one pound. And he looked at me and said, “Well, you surprise me. I guess it is hard for you to lose weight, isn’t it?” And I never saw him again.

The doctor may have attacked Doris and refused to trust her due to holding stigmatising beliefs and attitudes towards higher weight people. Bruce G. Link and Jo C. Phelan (2001) describe stigma as a social process where differences between people are labelled, where people are associated with negative stereotypes, where people are separated into “us” and “them”, and where people experience discrimination, and a loss of social status. Doris’ attack is an instance of direct, individual discrimination. However, there are many more processes through which weight stigma exists and operates.

Bruce G. Link and Jo C. Phelan (2001) also observe that “all manner of disadvantage can result from outside of a model in which one person does something bad to another”. They give the examples of institutional racism, disabling environments, and mental health stigma, to demonstrate how structural discrimination, or structural stigma, occurs in specific groups. Mark L. Hatzenbuehler and Bruce G. Link (2014) define structural stigma as “societal-level conditions, cultural norms, and institutional policies that constrain the opportunities, resources, and wellbeing of the stigmatized”.

Bek J. Orr (2022) shares one of their experiences of structural discrimination as a fat trans non-binary person:

I sat on the narrow, paper-covered examination table, shirtless and exposed to the nurse practitioner as she took my breast measurements and brusquely asked me what my expectations were for breast reduction. For months I had practiced this very conversation with my partner and alone, yet I was still nervous and inarticulate. It was complicated. I fumbled through a nervous over-explanation of my gender identity and surgical outcome hopes. Without responding to my manic monologue, the nurse practitioner said I would be a good candidate for surgery covered by insurance. I felt the breath I didn’t know I was holding release and my heart surge! Then came the however. “However, your BMI exceeds our cutoff to proceed with surgery. Obesity is connected to surgical complication, so we’ll need you to bring your BMI down below 30, calculated to 40 pounds. You’ll need to lose 40 pounds and come back for another consultation with the surgeon before we can move forward.” As I walked out of the building I wondered out loud, “They’ve had all my records including my BMI since I made the appointment months ago. Why did they have me come and sit through the humiliation of that exam only to tell me that I couldn’t see the surgeon and to come back 40 pounds lighter? How quickly can I lose 40 pounds?” I felt deflated and ashamed.

In the weeks that followed I actively avoided thinking about the appointment, surgery that would not happen, the body that did not feel like mine. I felt frozen with shame and disappointment. Months later, when I was finally able to share my story with close friends, they insisted, that I should write about this. Only then, with the distance that academic research can provide, I started to outline what became this article.

In Bek’s case, they were refused care on the basis of a BMI cutoff policy, which presumably has been created because of perceived medical and medico-legal risks around surgeries for higher weight patients.

This is an example of a particular form of stigma called pathologisation, which Rachel Fox and others (2023) describe as a social process through which people are diagnosed with an illness, dehumanised by being understood through their illness, and then subjected to intervention in order to cure their illness. Doris also experienced pathologisation, as her doctor seemed to be concerned with the effect of her weight on the safety of her child; he attempted to reduce her weight through medical interventions; he attacked her after getting to a point where he no longer cared for her wellbeing; and he was unwilling or unable to trust Doris’ knowledge of her own body.

Pathologisation also manifests in the way Bek and Doris both describe experiences of shame and disconnection from their bodies. These feelings are connected to what is commonly called internalised stigma, and which Bruce G. Link and Jo C. Phelan (2001) refer to as the “social psychological processes operating through the stigmatized person”:

The person may wonder, “Will others look down on me, reject me, simply because I have been identified as having a mental illness?” Then to the extent that it becomes a part of a person’s world view, that perception can have serious negative consequences.

In the case of Bek and Doris’, both of them were apprehensive ahead of their appointments, and both showed an experience of psychological trauma which affected their self-worth and required time to recover from.

Figure 1 shows how these different forms of stigma manifest within Bek and Doris’ accounts. We represent part of each account as a card with a sequence of events running left to right. Above and below each card, we place events or processes which might influence, or be influenced by, the events in the account, so that time also runs ‘through’ the account from top to bottom. Doris talks about her fear of the weigh-in, and Bek mentions practicing their conversation with their partner. These behaviours may be indicative of prior negative experiences, and can be understood as examples of healthcare avoidance or hesitation. During their appointments, they each experience either interpersonal or structural stigma. These are themselves a result of pathologisation, as both Doris’ doctor and Bek’s nurse practicioner are constructing their weight as a risk factor which needs to be forcibly modified. All of this leads to strong negative emotions which can linger as internalised stigma. This internalisation may reinforce the existing disposition to healthcare avoidance and ultimately worsen health inequalities. In other words, if going to the doctor harms you, eventually you will stop going to the doctor.

Doris and Bek’s accounts show just some of the ways that different forms of weight stigma and other harmful social processes like healthcare avoidance can drive each other. These understandings about how weight stigma operates make it easier for us to see what things need to change, and where we can act to heal from, dismantle, and prevent weight stigma. In Bek’s case, we should ask why and how the BMI cutoff was implemented, so that we can understand how to remove that barrier from the surgery process. In Doris’ case, we should ask why the doctor was so concerned about her weight in relation to her pregnancy, and how he got to a point where he attacked his patient. In both cases, we should ask what people need to heal from traumatic experiences, to prevent them lingering and contributing to internalised stigma, and we should ask what we need to handle the things which lead to us avoiding accessing healthcare.

Activists and researchers continue to put a huge amount of effort into challenging weight stigma, as shown by the huge increase in academic publications about weight stigma over time. As of 12th February 2025, a search on Google Scholar for ("weight stigma" OR "sizeism" OR "fat bias" OR "weight bias" OR "fatphobia") yields “about 21,600 results”, with “about 17,400” of these in the last 10 years. Repeating this search on Google reveals that these academic publications form the majority of the results that people see when looking for information about, and accounts of people’s experiences of, weight stigma. We will discuss some of this work in a later article, “Existing interventions for challenging weight stigma”.

The aim of the Central Lancashire End Weight Stigma project is to enable us, the people of Central Lancashire, to heal from, dismantle, and prevent weight stigma in our local healthcare system. To learn more about our approach and get involved, visit our homepage.
